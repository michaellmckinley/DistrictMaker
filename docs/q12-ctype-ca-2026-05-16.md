# Q12 ctype probe on California — 2026-05-16

**Question (Q12 in [`open-questions.md`](open-questions.md)):** Does varying METIS's coarsening type from the default `SHEM` (sorted heavy-edge matching) to `RM` (random matching) broaden the reachable basin set, on the theory that random matching is far more stochastic and should carve genuinely different basins than fresh seeds alone can reach?

**Verdict: RM produces worse basins than SHEM, not just different ones.** 19 of 20 `metis+kl` trials on CA with `ctype=RM` produced population-feasible partitions (seed 47 failed METIS tolerance, paralleling SHEM's seed-46 failure). RM's best trial is **7794.58 km** (seed 53), which is **+13.08% longer** than SHEM's best of 6893.38 km. Every other RM summary statistic is worse, often dramatically: mean +29.6%, median +14.6%, std blows out from 287.72 km (3.87%) to 3406.25 km (35.32%). Two RM trials produced pathological outliers above 13,000 km. `ctype=RM` is not a viable Q12 lever; SHEM is the right default and the structural-diversification path will have to go through other knobs (or iterated local search).

## Setup

- State: CA (52 districts).
- Algorithm: `metis+kl` only (this experiment isolates the ctype variable; `splitline-realized+kl` is not re-run).
- ctype: `RM` (random matching) instead of the default `SHEM`. All other METIS options match the SHEM baseline: `tolerance=0.005`, `contiguous=True`, `recursive=False`, `ncuts=10`, `niter=50`.
- Trials: 20; seeds 42–61 (`seed_i = base_seed + i`), same seed range as the 2026-05-15 SHEM baseline so results pair cleanly per seed.
- Compute: 24-core / 192 GB host, 6 worker processes, no `--cpu` cap (this experiment used the standalone `scripts/q12_ctype_ca_experiment.py` runner rather than the `multi-start` CLI).
- Run executed in two attempts due to a power-out on the first: 11 trials completed in attempt 1 (`2026-05-16 07:10–07:21`), the remaining 9 trials (indices 3 and 12–19) ran in attempt 2 (`2026-05-16 09:10–10:50`). Total wall clock ~1h 41m for attempt 2 alone; attempt-1 trial outputs were reused unchanged.
- Verification gate at `scripts/q12_verify_ctype_plumbing.py` confirmed pre-experiment that `pymetis.Options().ctype` is actually plumbed through to METIS (SHEM and RM produced different partitions on a 100-node grid).

RM is worse on every metric. The std blow-up (3.87% → 35.32%) is the most striking number: SHEM's seed-driven variation is a narrow exploration around a stable basin, while RM scatters trials across a much wider quality range — with most of that scatter heading in the wrong direction. Two trials produced clear pathological outliers (trial-03-seed-45 at 22760.67 km and trial-18-seed-60 at 13351.39 km); removing them drops RM's std from 35.32% to roughly 5–6%, but RM's mean and best still trail SHEM substantially even after that adjustment.

The trial-05-seed-47 failure (`RuntimeError: METIS could not produce a partition within tolerance 0.0050; best achieved deviation was 0.0430`) is the RM analog of the SHEM seed-46 failure (deviation 0.0112). Same tolerance-feasibility failure mode at very-large states, different seed.

Two observations on the rankings:

- **Even RM's best trial trails SHEM's best by 13%.** This is not a "RM is comparable but distinct" pattern (which would have justified ensembling SHEM + RM); it's a "RM is meaningfully worse" pattern. No RM trial gets close to SHEM's 6893.38 km floor — the closest is +13%.
- **Pathological outliers correlate with extreme runtimes.** The two worst trials (seeds 45 and 60) both spent ~90–100 min in METIS's ufactor retry loop before eventually producing a poor-quality partition that satisfied tolerance. This is the RM coarsening repeatedly failing population balance and METIS bouncing through `ufactor in range(5, 0, -1)` before finally returning. SHEM's tight runtime variance (most trials in the same few-minute range) reflects coarsening that hits balance on the first attempt.

SHEM saturates at N=2 (seed 43) and stays put. RM continues to inch downward through N=20 (improvements land at N=2, N=7, and N=12), but at no checkpoint does it match or beat SHEM. RM's improvement-after-N=2 says it does explore more of the basin landscape than SHEM does — consistent with the random-matching theory — but the basins it reaches are uniformly worse than the SHEM-reachable set on this state.

The aggregator-generated `_summary.md` saturation table for this run shows a slightly different N=1 value than what's reported here; that file appears to compute N=1 from a different ordering. The numbers in this writeup are computed directly from `distributions.json` in seed order and are authoritative for the comparison.

## Q12 verdict

**`ctype=RM` is not a viable Q12 lever on CA.** The structural-diversification hypothesis — that varying coarsening type reaches genuinely different (and potentially better) basins than fresh seeds alone — is rejected for this specific knob on this specific state. RM does diversify the basin set (saturation continues past N=2 rather than locking at the SHEM floor), but it diversifies into worse territory, not better.

What this implies for the broader Q12 agenda from [`open-questions.md`](open-questions.md):

- **ctype variation should not be promoted to a first-class algorithm.** No reason to add `metis-rm+kl` to `ALGORITHM_NAMES` in `compare.py`, no reason to add `--metis-ctype` to the multi-start CLI. The SHEM default stays.
- **The other METIS structural options (`iptype`, `rtype`) are now lower-priority probes, not higher.** The original Q12 wording suggested ctype was the most promising of the METIS knobs. With ctype rejected as worse-not-different, the prior on the remaining structural options shifts downward; coarsening type is supposedly the most leveraged knob and yet didn't help. Iterated local search (Q12's second lever) is now the more promising remaining path.
- **The structural-diversification hypothesis itself is wounded but not killed.** SHEM may simply be a well-tuned default that already finds the right basin landscape; the failure isn't of "vary structure" generally but of "vary *coarsening type* specifically." Iterated local search perturbs a known-good partition rather than re-coarsening from scratch — it tests a different mechanism for reaching adjacent basins and is the natural next Q12 probe.

This experiment does not by itself promote or demote SHEM as the default; it only tests RM as a candidate alternative and finds it worse. The 6893.38 km CA leader remains unchanged.

## What this experiment did NOT settle

- **Whether the same pattern holds on TX.** TX has a different graph structure (38 districts vs CA's 52) and the ctype-effect may not transfer. The current data is one state only. If a TX probe is worth the wall-clock, run a 20-trial RM experiment there before drawing any cross-state conclusion. Cost estimate: similar order to this run (1–2h on the same hardware), but TX trials are individually faster.
- **Whether iterated local search (Q12's second lever) would help.** A different mechanism entirely — perturb-then-refine vs re-coarsen-then-refine — and the only remaining Q12 lever that hasn't been touched.
- **Whether `iptype` or `rtype` would also produce worse-not-different results.** The prior is now lower, but they're not directly tested. Cheap to test on small states first if the question recurs.
- **Whether the two pathological RM trials (seeds 45 and 60) reflect a deeper structural failure or just unlucky coarsening on those particular seeds.** Both spent ~100 min in the ufactor retry loop and ended at poor-quality partitions. Worth understanding only if RM ever comes back into scope; for now they're recorded and ignored.
- **Whether SHEM is the *globally* right default across all 44 states, not just the very-large ones.** This experiment compares RM to SHEM only on CA. SHEM's status as default is unchallenged by this experiment but also unconfirmed beyond CA.
